## Introduction

Loretta Afraid of Bear-Cook joins us in today’s conversation to talk about “Tiospaye” – a Lakota word for “extended family, community”. Loretta’s work circles around the Afraid of Bear – American Horse Tiospaye of the Oglala. 

Our conversation ranges over a large number of interconnected themes and topics, including:
 - Living in community
 - Traditional life experiences
 - Language and Mental worlds
 - Foods and seasons
 - Medicine, ceremony, prayer
 - Relations and relationality
 - Male / female relations
 - Rites of passage

Join us for a thoughtful and thought-provoking episode of Domination Chronicles.


## Speaker Introductions

 - **Peter P. d'Errico** (Co-Host): A researcher and writer with decades of experience in indigenous people's issues, specifically focusing on Federal Anti-Indian Law. He shares personal reflections on his Italian heritage and the impact of modernization on traditional food systems.

 - **Steven Newcomb** (Co-Host): An author and researcher of indigenous issues for over 45 years. He specializes in the study of colonization as a system of domination and the "linguicide" of native languages.

 - **Loretta Afraid of Bear-Cook** (Guest): An Oglala Lakota elder born in 1947 who was raised traditionally in her language and culture. She is a practitioner of Wolakota traditions and serves her community through rites of passage programs and the preservation of sacred knowledge.


## Conversation Summary

 - **Cultural Identity and Naming:** Loretta introduces herself by her Lakota name, Umpetulutawi, which describes the red stripe that appears at dawn. She emphasizes the importance of understanding one’s place and being in service to the community.

 - **Traditional Upbringing:** Loretta describes growing up in a community where childcare, food, and medicine were shared responsibilities. She was raised with Lakota as her first language, noting that it defines her "train of mind" in a way the "ambiguous" English language does not.

 - **Language and "Linguicide":** The speakers discuss how the imposition of English was a deliberate attempt to destroy indigenous cultures. Loretta notes the difficulty of translating deep Lakota concepts into English, which often lacks the same relational heart.

 - **Sacred Food and Seasons:** Loretta details the protocols for gathering traditional foods like Timsila (tubers) and Aoyampi (berries), explaining that these practices are tied to specific seasons and respect for other "relatives" like birds and animals.

 - **Rites of Passage:** The discussion explores ceremonies such as Ishnuti for girls and Ho-Trohk'echa for boys, which build character, responsibility, and spiritual connection through the creation of medicine bundles and the use of the eagle bone whistle.
    
 - **Modern Challenges:** The group critiques "convenience" and the industrial food system, noting how they disrupt the sacred relationship between humans and the earth. Loretta also expresses caution regarding AI, sharing a negative experience where technology jumbled a sacred prayer.


## Important Terms and Subjects
 - **Wolakota:** The traditional Lakota way of life and harmony.

 - **Linguicide:** The intentional killing or destruction of a language.

 - **Timsila:** Wild prairie tubers gathered specifically in the month of June.

 - **Aoyampi:** The first berries of the spring and summer.

 - **Dakushkashka:** A term for the all-powerful being, representing sacred movement and everything alive.

 - **Wo'op'cheh:** The laws and principles specific to Lakota/Oglala women.

 - **Ishnuti:** A rite of passage ceremony for young women focused on discernment and responsibility.

  - **Ho-Trohk'echa:** A rite of passage for young boys, marking the "changing of the voice".

 - **Eagle Bone Whistle:** A sacred instrument used to convey a spiritual calling or sound.

 - **Language Nest:** An immersion program where children and families learn their native language from a very young age.
